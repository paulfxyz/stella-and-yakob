About

Why this book?

Children are growing up in a world where AI is everywhere — and often, the adults around them are just as confused. Stella & Yakob was born from a simple belief: that even the hardest questions can be explored gently, with wonder and honesty.

The book doesn't preach or simplify. It acknowledges that there are no easy answers. But it gives children — and the grown-ups who read to them — a language for curiosity, discernment, and the irreplaceable things that make us human.

— Paul Fleury

Inspiration

The spirit of
Le Petit Prince

I have been obsessed with Le Petit Prince since I was a child. Not because it is a children's book — though it is — but because it is one of the most quietly devastating pieces of philosophy ever written, disguised as a story about a boy, a fox, and a rose.

Antoine de Saint-Exupéry was a pioneer aviator who flew mail planes over the Sahara in the 1930s. He understood what it meant to be completely alone in the dark, to depend on instruments — on machines — and yet to know that machines would never tell him anything about what actually mattered. He disappeared over the Mediterranean in 1944. He was 44. He left behind one small book that has sold over 200 million copies in 300 languages.

His central conviction was that the most important things in life are invisible to purely rational, mechanical analysis. You cannot measure love. You cannot optimise wonder. You cannot calculate the value of a rose tamed by a child.

Stella & Yakob is my attempt to carry that torch into the age of artificial intelligence. I guided the AI to illustrate this story — every image started from a human vision, expressed through a machine, reaching for something Saint-Exupéry would have recognised: the essential thing that is invisible to the eyes.

Whether we succeeded is for you to say.

— Paul Fleury, 2026
